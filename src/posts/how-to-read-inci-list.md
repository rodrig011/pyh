INCI lists look intimidating, but they follow one strict rule that, once you know it, tells you almost everything about a formula's priorities.

## The rule: descending order, with one exception

Ingredients are listed in order of concentration, from highest to lowest — **above 1%**. Once an ingredient drops below 1% concentration, brands are legally allowed to list the remaining ingredients in *any* order. That's why you'll often see a cluster of "good stuff" — niacinamide, peptides, extracts — bunched up at the end, in seemingly random order. It doesn't mean they're equally dosed; it just means they're all under 1%.

## What this means for you

- The first 4–5 ingredients usually make up 80%+ of the formula. If "water" and a couple of emollients dominate that list, the product is mostly a moisturizing base.
- A star ingredient buried near the end, after preservatives and fragrance, is almost certainly there at a low, possibly cosmetic, dose — not necessarily a useless dose, but not the headline percentage you might assume from the marketing.
- Fragrance ("parfum") can legally hide dozens of individual aroma compounds under one word, which is a separate transparency issue worth its own post.

## A quick trick

Find "phenoxyethanol," a common preservative usually used around 0.5–1%. Everything listed *after* it is below that threshold. It's not a perfect rule of thumb, but it's a genuinely useful anchor point when you're trying to estimate how much of a hyped active you're actually getting.
