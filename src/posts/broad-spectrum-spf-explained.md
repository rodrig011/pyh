SPF only tells you part of the story. The number measures protection against UVB rays — the ones that cause sunburn. "Broad spectrum" is the part of the label that tells you whether you're also covered for UVA.

## UVA vs UVB, quickly

- **UVB** is higher energy, mostly hits the surface, and is the main driver of sunburn — this is what SPF measures.
- **UVA** penetrates deeper into the dermis, doesn't cause visible burning the same way, and is the bigger contributor to photoaging and a factor in skin cancer risk. It also passes through window glass, which SPF labeling alone won't warn you about.

A high SPF number with no "broad spectrum" claim can leave you burn-free but still taking real UVA damage.

## Chemical vs mineral filters

Chemical filters (like avobenzone or octocrylene) absorb UV radiation and convert it to heat. Mineral filters (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) primarily reflect and scatter UV, though modern formulations of both work more like a blended physical-and-chemical system than the old "sits on top vs. absorbs" explanation suggests.

## PA++++ : the UVA grading system

In Asian sunscreen standards, you'll see "PA" ratings (PA+ through PA++++) measuring UVA protection specifically — more pluses means stronger UVA defense. The US label system doesn't use PA ratings, which is part of why "broad spectrum" is the closest equivalent claim you'll find domestically.

The takeaway: don't just chase the SPF number. "Broad spectrum" plus reapplication every two hours does more for your long-term skin health than SPF 100 with no UVA coverage ever will.
